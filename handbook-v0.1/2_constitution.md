# Constitution

## Law 01 — Curiosity Comes Before Knowledge
### Statement
Every topic begins with a question before it begins with an answer.
### Why this law exists
Questions create purpose. Learning becomes much easier and more meaningful when each concept solves a problem instead of being presented as isolated information. Questions also encourage active thinking rather than passive memorization.
### Practical consequence
Whenever beginning a new topic, write down the question it answers before studying the explanation.
## Law 02 — Understand Before Memorizing
### Statement
Understanding is the foundation; memorization is only a consequence.
### Why this law exists
Memorized information is easily forgotten or misapplied. Understanding allows knowledge to be reconstructed even when details are forgotten.
### Practical consequence
Whenever possible, explain a concept in your own words before attempting to memorize formulas, definitions, or procedures.
## Law 03 — Everything Must Connect
### Statement
Knowledge should never exist in isolation.
### Why this law exists
Engineering is built from connections between disciplines. Every new concept should strengthen an existing mental model rather than become another disconnected fact.
### Practical consequence
Every new concept should be linked to previous concepts, future applications, or related research areas.
## Law 04 — Always Search for First Principles
### Statement
Before accepting how something works, understand why it exists.
### Why this law exists
First principles provide a stable foundation for solving unfamiliar problems and adapting knowledge to new situations.
### Practical consequence
Ask "Why?" before asking "How?" whenever studying a new concept.
## Law 05 — Theory Must Meet Reality
### Statement
Every theoretical concept should eventually be connected to a real engineering or medical problem.
### Why this law exists
Knowledge becomes more durable and meaningful when it is associated with practical situations and real-world challenges.
### Practical consequence
Whenever learning a concept, identify at least one real application where it is essential.
## Law 06 — Learn Systems, Not Isolated Parts
### Statement
Always understand how individual components contribute to the complete system.
### Why this law exists
Medical robots are complex systems composed of hardware, software, algorithms, and human interaction. Understanding only individual parts limits the ability to design or improve the whole.
### Practical consequence
Regularly ask how the current topic interacts with other components of a robotic system.
## Law 07 — Progress Is Measured by Ability
### Statement
Learning is measured by what can be explained, applied, and improved—not by the number of completed chapters.
### Why this law exists
Completing a course does not necessarily indicate mastery. Practical ability is a more reliable indicator of understanding.
### Practical consequence
Periodically evaluate knowledge through explanations, projects, problem solving, or teaching.
## Law 08 — Uncertainty Is a Guide
### Statement
Confusion identifies the next opportunity for learning.
### Why this law exists
Moments of uncertainty reveal gaps in understanding and provide direction for future study.
### Practical consequence
Instead of avoiding confusing topics, record them as questions to investigate later.

## Law 09 — Build Before Expanding
### Statement
Strong foundations support advanced knowledge.
### Why this law exists
Advanced robotics depends on mathematics, programming, physics, and engineering fundamentals. Weak foundations eventually limit progress.
### Practical consequence
When progress becomes difficult, verify whether missing prerequisites are responsible before continuing.
## Law 10 — Knowledge Evolves
### Statement
Understanding is expected to change over time.
### Why this law exists
Research constantly refines existing ideas. A growing engineer should be willing to replace incomplete mental models with better ones.
### Practical consequence
Record significant changes in understanding rather than hiding previous misconceptions.
## Law 11 — Learn Actively
### Statement
Knowledge grows through interaction, not observation alone.
### Why this law exists
Explaining, questioning, experimenting, and solving problems develop deeper understanding than passive reading.
### Practical consequence
Every study session should include at least one active activity, such as solving a problem, writing a summary, or asking new questions.
## Law 12 — Simplicity Before Complexity
### Statement
Prefer the simplest explanation that accurately describes a concept.
### Why this law exists
Simple models create intuition. Complexity should only be introduced when it genuinely improves understanding.
### Practical consequence
Always seek an intuitive explanation before studying formal mathematical models.
## Law 13 — Think Like an Engineer
### Statement
Every concept should be evaluated in terms of purpose, limitations, trade-offs, and implementation.
### Why this law exists
Engineering is not only about knowing solutions but also about understanding why certain solutions are chosen over others.
### Practical consequence
When studying a method or technology, ask what problem it solves, what assumptions it makes, and what limitations it has.
## Law 14 — Think Like a Researcher
### Statement
Knowledge is never complete.
### Why this law exists
Every solved problem reveals new questions. Research advances by identifying limitations and opportunities for improvement.
### Practical consequence
Whenever learning a mature concept, ask what researchers are currently trying to improve.
## Law 15 — Never Stop Building the Handbook
### Statement
The handbook is part of the learning process, not a record of its end.
### Why this law exists
A living knowledge system should evolve alongside its author. Its value comes from continuous refinement rather than initial completeness.
### Practical consequence
Review, reorganize, and improve the handbook whenever new understanding makes it possible.
