# Question Journey

> [!quote] How do I progress from knowing nothing to becoming capable of researching and developing medical robotics? 

## Purpose
- The Question Journey is the primary learning path of the handbook.
Instead of organizing knowledge by subjects or university courses, it organizes learning through increasingly complex questions. Each question represents a real challenge that an engineer or researcher may eventually need to answer.
The objective is not to finish chapters but to continuously develop the ability to answer better questions. Every answer should naturally generate new questions, making learning an ongoing process rather than a sequence of isolated lessons.
The Question Journey serves as the engine of the entire handbook. The other maps exist to support and enrich this journey.
## Description
- The Question Journey is composed of **Acts**, each representing a major stage in the development of a future medical robotics researcher.
Each Act contains progressively deeper questions. Questions may introduce concepts from multiple disciplines simultaneously because real engineering problems rarely belong to a single subject.
An Act is considered complete when its essential questions can be answered confidently and connected to the broader knowledge system.
The journey is expected to evolve over time. New questions, Acts, and refinements may be added as the handbook grows.

## Structure

```
Question Journey
	↓
Acts
	↓
Themes
	↓
Questions
	↓
Concepts
	↓
Knowledge Notes
	↓
Reflection
```

Notice something important:
**Questions lead to concepts—not the other way around.**

## Rules
### Rule 1
Every Act begins with one central question.
### Rule 2
Every question should have a clear purpose.
If a question does not contribute to becoming a better engineer or researcher, reconsider its inclusion.
### Rule 3
Questions should become progressively more difficult.
The journey should feel like climbing a mountain, not wandering randomly.
### Rule 4
A question may require knowledge from several disciplines.
The journey is interdisciplinary by design.
### Rule 5
Never introduce a concept before there is a question that justifies it.
### Rule 6
Questions should encourage reasoning rather than memorization.
> Prefer:
> _"Why does inverse kinematics have multiple solutions?"_
> instead of
> _"What is inverse kinematics?"_
### Rule 7
Each question should connect to at least one real engineering or medical problem.
### Rule 8
When a question cannot yet be answered because of missing prerequisites, record it and link it to the Dependency Graph rather than removing it.
### Rule 9
Each completed Act should naturally generate the motivation for the next Act.
The journey should feel continuous.
### Rule 10
The journey is never finished.
New questions are signs of progress, not failure.

## Acts
### Act I — What is Medical Robotics?
Fundamental questions:
- What is a robot?
- What makes a robot "medical"?
- Why are medical robots needed?
- What problems are they trying to solve?
- What are their limitations?
- What research areas exist?
### Act II — How Does a Robot Exist?
Questions like:
- What components make up a robot?
- What is a sensor?
- What is an actuator?
- What is a controller?
- How does information flow through the robot?
- How does software interact with hardware?
### Act III — How Does a Robot Move?
- Motion
- Kinematics
- Dynamics
- Control
- Planning
### Act IV — How Does a Robot Perceive?
- Cameras
- Sensors
- Computer Vision
- Sensor Fusion
- Medical Imaging
### Act V — How Does a Robot Think?
- Algorithms
- Artificial Intelligence
- Decision Making
- Learning
- Autonomy
### Act VI — How Does a Robot Interact?
- Surgeons
- Patients
- Researchers
- Human-Robot Interaction
- Haptics
### Act VII — How Do We Trust a Robot?
- Safety
- Verification
- Validation
- Ethics
- Regulations
- Reliability
### Act VIII — How Do We Create the Future?
- Scientific Research
- Reading Papers
- Designing Experiments
- Innovation
- Emerging Technologies
